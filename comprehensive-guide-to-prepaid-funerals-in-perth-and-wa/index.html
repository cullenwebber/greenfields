A Comprehensive Guide to Prepaid Funerals in Perth and WA

April 20, 2025

Yet, planning ahead for the end of life can be one of the most thoughtful and responsible actions we take for our families. It‚Äôs an act of love, providing clarity and easing burdens during an inevitably difficult time. Taking steps today allows you to maintain control over your final wishes and offers profound peace of mind, knowing that arrangements are in place.

Pre-planning a funeral involves making decisions now about the type of service you‚Äôd prefer, ensuring your farewell reflects your life and values. One practical way to achieve this is through a prepaid funeral plan. This allows you to not only outline your specific wishes but also to pay for the funeral director‚Äôs services in advance, often at today‚Äôs prices.

Making these arrangements now can significantly reduce future stress for your loved ones, allowing them space to grieve without the added pressure of making complex decisions or facing unexpected financial strain.

What Exactly is a Prepaid Funeral?

A prepaid funeral is essentially a contract between you and a chosen funeral director, such as Greenfields Funerals. Through this legally binding agreement, you select the specific funeral services and arrangements you want, and you pay for them in advance, locking in the cost at today’s prices for those agreed-upon elements.1 This means the core components of your funeral, as detailed in the contract, are secured financially, regardless of future price increases for those specific services.

The process involves documenting your preferences ‚Äì perhaps the type of service, whether burial or cremation, the style of coffin or casket, and other details ‚Äì and agreeing on the total cost with the funeral director. Payment can be made as a single lump sum or through installments over an agreed period, making it manageable for different budgets.A critical aspect of prepaid funerals, particularly under Australian regulations, concerns the security of your funds. When you pay for a prepaid funeral, the money isn’t simply held by the funeral director in their business account. In Western Australia, strict rules apply to protect consumers. For contracts entered into in WA after 1 March 2021, the funds you pay (excluding any initial administration fees) must be transferred by the funeral director to an independent, approved investment manager within 16 days of receipt.8 This money is then invested securely in your name. These funds are kept separate from the funeral home’s business assets, safeguarding your investment even if the funeral business were to close down. The investment can only be accessed by the funeral director after the agreed-upon funeral service has been provided, following notification from the funeral organiser. This separation between the funeral director providing the service and the independent entity managing the funds is a key protection mechanism designed to give you confidence that the money set aside for your funeral is secure.

Why Consider a Prepaid Funeral?

Choosing a prepaid funeral offers several significant advantages, providing emotional and financial reassurance for you and your family.

Locking in Costs & Beating Inflation

One of the most compelling financial benefits is fixing the cost of funeral services at today’s prices. Funeral costs in Australia have shown a consistent trend of rising, often outpacing general inflation. Costs can range significantly, typically from around $4,000 for a basic cremation to $15,000 or more for elaborate burials, depending on choices and location. For example, the Australian Seniors Cost of Death Report noted average burial costs rising from around $9,055 in 2019 to $11,039 by 2023. By prepaying, you protect your family from these future price increases for the specific services included in your contract.

Easing the Burden on Loved Ones

Perhaps the most significant emotional benefit is relieving your family of difficult decisions and financial worries during a time of grief. When a death occurs, arranging a funeral involves numerous choices under emotional distress and time pressure. A prepaid plan provides clear instructions and ensures the funds are available, allowing your loved ones to focus on remembering and supporting each other. It removes the guesswork and potential disagreements about what you would have wanted.

Ensuring Your Wishes Are Respected

A prepaid funeral allows you to plan your farewell, your way. You can specify details that are important to you ‚Äì burial or cremation, the type of service, coffin or casket selection (including eco-friendly options), music, flowers, and readings. Documenting these choices ensures your final arrangements align with your personal values and preferences, providing comfort that your wishes will be honoured. While this provides control, involving family members in the pre-planning discussion can also be a meaningful way to ensure the arrangements feel right for everyone when the time comes.

Potential Pension Advantages

For individuals receiving or planning for the Age Pension or other Centrelink/DVA benefits, prepaid funerals offer a notable advantage regarding asset testing. Once a prepaid funeral plan is fully paid for, the amount invested is generally exempt from the Centrelink and DVA assets test, and there is no upper limit on this exemption. This means you can set aside funds for your desired funeral without impacting your pension eligibility. This differs significantly from funeral bonds, which have a capped exemption limit (currently around $15,500, indexed annually). It’s always advisable to confirm your specific circumstances with Services Australia.

What’s Involved in Planning a Prepaid Funeral?

Navigating funeral costs can feel complex, as prices vary widely based on location, provider, and the specific choices made. As mentioned, typical funerals in Australia can range from $4,000 to over $15,000. Greenfields Funerals is committed to providing affordable, quality services, with options available starting from $3,825

Understanding the components that make up the total cost is helpful when planning:

Funeral Director Professional Service Fees

This covers the funeral director’s expertise in coordinating all arrangements, handling legal paperwork, liaising with third parties, and the time of their staff. Fees typically range from $2,000 to $7,000+.

Transport

Transferring the deceased into the funeral home’s care. Costs around $300-$450.

Mortuary Care

Preparation and care of the deceased prior to the funeral. Basic care is standard; embalming is usually optional and incurs an additional fee ($600-$1400).

Coffin or Casket

This is often one of the most significant variable costs. Options range from simple, practical coffins (around $800) to elaborate, high-end caskets costing $10,000 or more. Eco-friendly cardboard or timber options are also available.

Venue Hire

Costs associated with using a chapel, church, or other venue for the service.

Burial or Cremation Costs

Burial

Includes the purchase of the burial plot (which can be extremely expensive, especially in metropolitan areas like Perth, potentially ranging from $4,000 to $15,000+) and interment fees (opening and closing the grave, around $1,500-$3,000). These plot costs are a major factor in why burials are often more expensive than cremations.

Cremation

Includes the cremation fee charged by the crematorium and necessary permits/certificates. Cremation services generally range from $3,100 to over $7,000 depending on inclusions.

Celebrant or Clergy Fees

 Payment for the person officiating the service ($250-$1,000).

Certificates and Permits

Costs for the official death certificate and any burial/cremation permits.

Optional Extras

These can add significantly to the total cost and include flowers ($200-$1,500+), newspaper death notices ($100-$800+), catering for a wake ($500-$3,000+), mourning stationery (orders of service, thank you cards), hearse and other funeral vehicles, and a headstone or memorial plaque ($2,000-$10,000+).

A prepaid funeral contract will clearly specify which of these costs are included in the fixed price. Typically, this covers the core services provided directly by the funeral director (professional fees, transport, basic mortuary care, the selected coffin/casket, and the arrangement of the burial or cremation service itself).

It is crucial to understand what might not be covered. Often, costs payable to third parties (known as disbursements), such as the burial plot fee, cremation fees charged by the crematorium, celebrant fees, death certificate costs, flowers, catering, or the headstone, may be excluded unless specifically itemised and included in the contract total. Always review the contract carefully to confirm exactly what is included in the prepaid price to avoid unexpected expenses for your family later.

Weighing Your Options

While a prepaid funeral offers distinct advantages, it’s one of several ways Australians plan for final expenses. Other approaches include setting up a dedicated savings account or term deposit (though this doesn’t lock in prices or offer the same asset protection), potentially using funds from superannuation (access rules apply and may require upfront payment by family), or relying on government bereavement payments (eligibility criteria apply).

However, the main financial products specifically designed for funeral planning are prepaid funerals, funeral bonds, and funeral insurance. Understanding their differences is key to making an informed choice.

Prepaid Funerals

 As detailed earlier, their strength lies in providing certainty. You know the arrangements are made and the core costs are covered. The unlimited Centrelink asset test exemption is a key advantage for some. However, they can be less flexible if your circumstances or wishes change significantly, or if you move away from the funeral director’s service area ‚Äì although some plans offer transferability. Choosing a reputable provider is important.

Funeral Bonds

These are essentially managed investment funds designed solely for funeral expenses. You contribute funds, which are invested and aim to grow over time, although returns are not guaranteed and have recently been modest. The accumulated amount (up to an indexed cap, currently around $15,500) is exempt from the Centrelink/DVA assets test. Bonds offer flexibility, as the funds can typically be used with any funeral director chosen by your family at the time. However, they do not lock in the price of the funeral, so if costs rise faster than the bond grows, there may be a shortfall.  Fees may also apply. They are regulated by APRA and ASIC.

Funeral Insurance

This is an insurance policy, not a savings plan. You pay regular premiums for a predetermined lump sum payout (typically $3,000-$15,000) upon death. The main concern is cost-effectiveness. Premiums, especially ‘stepped’ premiums, often increase significantly as you age, potentially becoming unaffordable. ASIC has warned that many people end up paying far more in premiums than the final benefit amount, particularly if they live for many years after taking out the policy.  Cancellation rates are very high (around 80% according to ASIC), and if you cancel or miss payments, you typically lose all the money paid. While some policies offer features like level premiums, premium caps after a certain age (e.g., 85), or early cash-out options, the fundamental risk of overpayment remains high. Funeral insurance may provide cover quickly if death occurs shortly after taking out the policy, but requires careful consideration due to the significant long-term cost risks. It is regulated by ASIC.

The best option depends entirely on your personal priorities ‚Äì whether you value price certainty and specific arrangements (Prepaid Funeral), flexible saving with some tax advantages (Funeral Bond), or are seeking immediate cover despite potential long-term costs (Funeral Insurance, approached with caution).

Understanding Regulations in WA

When considering a prepaid funeral, it‚Äôs natural to want reassurance that your investment and wishes are protected. Fortunately, Australia has regulations in place, with Western Australia offering specific, robust protections for consumers entering into prepaid funeral contracts.

Fund Security Across Australia

A key protection across most states (including WA, NSW, QLD, SA, VIC, TAS) is the requirement for funeral directors to place the funds received for prepaid funerals into independently managed, secure investments.4 These funds are held separately from the funeral director‚Äôs business accounts, typically in registered funeral funds or with approved investment managers.8 This segregation is designed to protect your money should the funeral business encounter financial difficulty or cease trading.7 It’s worth noting that consumer protections are generally considered less stringent in the ACT and Northern Territory.

National Oversight

Nationally, the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC) oversees fair trading practices, ensuring contracts don’t contain unfair terms and that businesses do not engage in misleading or deceptive conduct regarding funeral services.44 The Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC) regulates financial products like funeral bonds and funeral insurance, and provides valuable consumer information through its MoneySmart website.

Specific Protections in Western Australia

Western Australia has implemented specific codes of practice to provide strong consumer safeguards for prepaid funerals arranged from 1 March 2021 onwards. Dealing with a reputable provider like Greenfields Funerals ensures compliance with these important regulations.

Fair Trading (Prepaid Funerals Code of Practice) Regulations 2020: This code sets strict rules for prepaid funeral contracts:

  • Fund Management: Funds (less allowable fees) must be sent to an independent investment manager within 16 days of receipt.
  • Investment Security: The investment must be held in the client’s name and can only be released to the funeral director after the agreed funeral has been provided.
  • Contract Clarity: Contracts must be in clear, readable print (at least 12-point font), signed by both parties, dated, and must clearly identify the funeral organiser, the client, the recipient (if different), a contact person, the investment manager, and details of the agreed services and costs.
  • Fixed Price Guarantee: The price for the services specified in the contract is fixed and cannot be increased by the funeral director later, unless both parties agree to vary the contract (e.g., to add extra services).
  • Contingency Planning: The contract must outline procedures for situations like the funeral director closing down, missed payments (if applicable), or contract cancellation.
  • Cooling-Off Period: A mandatory 30-day cooling-off period applies.

Fair Trading (Funeral Pricing Code of Practice) Regulations 2022: 

This code focuses on transparency in funeral pricing generally:

  • Price Display: Funeral directors must prominently display clear, itemised price lists for their goods and services (including a basic funeral package option) on their website (if they have one) and at their business premises.
  • Itemised Quote: Before a consumer agrees to any funeral arrangement (prepaid or at-need), the funeral director must provide a written, itemised quote detailing all costs and the total amount payable.

These two codes work together to ensure that consumers in Western Australia receive clear information about costs and services, and that funds paid for prepaid funerals are securely managed. If you have any concerns or issues with a prepaid funeral provider in WA, you can contact Consumer Protection WA. While these regulations provide a strong safety net, choosing a funeral director with a proven history of ethical practice and community trust, like Greenfields Funerals, adds another layer of assurance.

Important Things to Keep in Mind When Planning a Prepaid Funeral

While prepaid funerals offer many benefits and strong protections, especially in WA, there are a few practical points to consider before finalising your plan:

  • Contract Flexibility and Transferability: Prepaid funeral contracts lock in specific services at a set price, which provides certainty but can sometimes mean less flexibility if your circumstances change. It’s important to ask about:
  • Transferability: What happens if you move interstate or even to a different part of WA outside Greenfields Funerals’ service area (Perth and Southwest WA)? Some providers allow plans to be transferred to another funeral home, potentially with associated fees, while others may not.2 Clarify the policy on transferring your plan before you sign.
  • Changing Details: If your preferences for minor details change over time, does the contract allow for amendments? While the core price is fixed, understanding the process for any potential variations is wise.
  • Provider Stability and Reputation: Although WA regulations require funds to be held independently, the ultimate delivery of the funeral service relies on the funeral director. Choosing a provider with a strong reputation, financial stability, and a long-standing presence in the community, like Greenfields Funerals (a proudly family-owned and locally operated business), provides additional peace of mind and reduces the risks associated with potential business closure or mismanagement, which unfortunately have occurred elsewhere in the industry. Positive client testimonials can also be a good indicator of reliable service.
  • Ensuring Comprehensive Coverage: Carefully consider everything you wish to be included in your funeral plan. To avoid potential unexpected costs for your family, ensure the contract clearly lists all covered items. Discuss explicitly whether elements like the burial plot purchase, interment fees, headstone or plaque, specific celebrant fees, flowers, or catering are included in the prepaid price or if there will be additional expenses at the time of the funeral. Clarity upfront prevents confusion later.
  • Communication with Family: Once you have arranged your prepaid funeral, it’s essential to inform your next of kin or executor. Let them know that the plan exists, which funeral director holds it (Greenfields Funerals), and where the documents are kept. This ensures they can easily activate the plan when the time comes.
